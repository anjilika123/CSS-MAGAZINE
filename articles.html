1. Digital Responsibility

Digital citizenship begins with responsibility. Every action online has consequences.

Digital responsibility refers to the obligation of people to use and share technology in a responsible, ethical and safe way.


Use proper language and behavior when interacting online. This helps prevent cyberharassment.

Respect other people’s opinions. Listen and think carefully before giving your own view.

Follow the rules of each website.

Report cyber threats and misuse of digital resources.

2. Preventing Cyberbullying

Cyberbullying affects many teenagers today.

How can we prevent cyberbullying?

Educate Yourself: Learn what cyberbullying is and where it happens. Talk to friends about what they see online.

Protect Your Password: Keep your passwords private. Do not share them with anyone, and change them if needed.

Think Before Posting Photos: Do not share photos you would not want everyone to see. They can be used to hurt you.

Avoid Unknown Messages: Do not open messages or links from people you do not know. They may contain viruses or steal your information.

Log Out of Accounts: Always log out, especially on shared devices. Do not save passwords on public computers.

3. Online Privacy

Protecting personal data is essential.

Cookies and web tracking: Set your browser to delete cookies every time you finish browsing or set "opt out" cookies on your browser to cookies aren't allowed at all in your browser.

IP address tracking: Request a private WHOIS listing through Network Solutions to hide your contact details. Use a VPN like IPVanish to encrypt your IP address and route traffic securely. Home IP addresses usually stay the same until you power off your modem.

The threat from the cloud: Confirm who handles each cloud security control, manage access using the provider’s identity tools, ensure your data is encrypted, and enable logging to monitor for unauthorized access.

4. Ethical Use of Information

Ethical use of information shows integrity.

Cite sources properly: Proper citation gives credit to original authors and helps prevent plagiarism when using others’ work or ideas.

Respect for intellectual property: Intellectual property protects creators’ rights through copyrights, patents, and trademarks, and ethical use means not using protected material without permission.

Accuracy and reliability: Ethical information use means relying on credible, accurate sources and verifying facts before using them.

5. Digital Footprint

Your digital footprint represents your online identity.

Why does this matter?

A digital footprint can determine a person’s digital reputation, which is now considered as important as their offline reputation.

Words and photos which you post online can be misinterpreted or altered, causing unintentional offense.

Content intended for a private group can spread to a broader circle, potentially damaging relationships and friendships.

6. Digital Citizenship in Schools

Schools play a role in shaping digital behavior.

In education, digital citizenship teaches students to use technology ethically, responsibly, and effectively.


What are the components of Digital Citienship?

Digital Literacy: The ability to use technology, manage information, and evaluate online content critically.

Digital Etiquette: Responsible online behavior means respecting others, avoiding cyberbullying, and understanding the impact of your actions.

Digital Ethics: Understanding legal and ethical rules, including copyright, digital rights, and proper software use.

Digital Responsibilities: Knowing your rights to privacy, speech, and information while respecting others’ rights and privacy.

Digital Wellness: Understanding how digital technology affects health and adopting safe, healthy usage habits.

Online Safety: Practicing safe technology use by securing passwords, protecting personal data, and avoiding cyber threats.
